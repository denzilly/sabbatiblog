Twenty-three days in Japan's forgotten north. No Shinkansen crowds, no queues at dawn. Just cedar forests older than the Edo period, onsen carved out of volcanic rock, and coastline that still feels like a discovery. Late May into June is arguably the finest time — the mountains have just shaken off their snow, the beech forests burn vivid green, and the tourist masses are still weeks away.

01 · Days 1–2

Sendai

Miyagi Prefecture · Gateway city

The obvious entry point from China — direct or one-stop flights into Sendai Airport. Use these two days to decompress and reset timezones before the real journey begins.

The Zuihoden mausoleum, a baroque-era monument to feudal lord Date Masamune, is worth a morning. The Kokubuncho district is the best street-food and craft sake corridor in the city.

02 · Days 3–4

Matsushima

Miyagi Prefecture · One of Japan's Three Views

A bay scattered with 260 pine-topped islands, classified alongside Miyajima and Amanohashidate as one of Japan's three most celebrated views. The boat cruise between the islands at golden hour is quietly spectacular.

Zuiganji temple — rebuilt in 1609 by Date Masamune — sits directly on the waterfront. The cave corridors carved into the cliff behind it are strange and worth the detour.

03 · Days 5–8

Dewa Sanzan

Yamagata Prefecture · Sacred mountains

Three mountains representing birth, death and rebirth — the spiritual core of Tohoku, a pilgrimage site for over 1,400 years. Yamabushi mountain monks still train here. You can feel the weight of it.

Mt Haguro: the cedar forest approach with its 2,446 stone steps, then an overnight in a shukubo lodge at the summit. Mt Gassan: a snow-covered alpine ascent (microspikes needed in late May) — the summit shrine is closed until July but the approach is extraordinary. Mt Yudono opens June 1 and is the most sacred of the three — photography forbidden, shoes removed at the gate.

⚠ Gas-san summit shrine closed until July 1 — but the ascent itself, through snowfields and alpine meadows, is the reason to go.

04 · Days 9–11

Nyuto Onsen

Akita Prefecture · Mountain hot springs

Seven ryokan scattered through a beech forest at the foot of Mt Nyuto — each fed by a different spring, each with its own character. This is the Tohoku onsen experience: no resort hotels, no lobby bars, just thatched roofs and sulphur steam.

Tsurunoyu, the oldest at over 350 years, is the one to aim for: milky-white water in an outdoor bath surrounded by forest, irori hearth dinners, oil-lamp rooms without television. The Yumeguri Pass lets you hop between all seven onsen in a day.

Book Tsurunoyu exactly 6 months in advance — phone only, from 7am on the 1st of the month. Main rooms go fast; the Yama no Yado annex is the fallback.

05 · Days 12–13

Tazawa & Kakunodate

Akita Prefecture · Lake & samurai town

Lake Tazawa is Japan's deepest lake — an intense, almost cobalt blue from the minerals in the water. It sits right on the Akita Shinkansen line, making it a natural stop between Nyuto and Aomori.

Kakunodate, 20 minutes south, is everything Kyoto's preserved districts used to be before the crowds arrived. Samurai houses line the main street, many still owned by the same families after 400 years. In late May, weeping cherry trees overhang the black-walled kura storehouses.

06 · Days 14–16

Oirase Gorge

Aomori Prefecture · Forest stream walk

A 14-kilometre path alongside one of the most photographed streams in Japan: crystal water rushing between moss-carpeted boulders, a dozen named waterfalls, and a canopy that turns the light jade-green in June. Flat, well-maintained, deeply calming.

Mid-May to June is one of the two ideal seasons — the fresh greenery is at its most intense and the autumn crowds are months away. Walk the full route in around 4–5 hours, or do a section and take the bus back. The gorge ends at Lake Towada, where you can stay overnight and do a boat cruise on the caldera lake the following morning.

07 · Days 17–18

Mt Hakkoda

Aomori Prefecture · Volcanic ridge

A cluster of volcanic peaks between Towada and Aomori city — famous for receiving some of the heaviest snowfall of any mountain in the world (up to 17 metres in winter). By June, the ropeway is running and the ridgeline trails are open, offering wide views across Aomori Bay and back towards the Towada plateau.

A good half-day from Aomori city before settling in for the final stretch. The Sukayu Onsen at the mountain base is one of the last remaining large mixed-gender public baths (sennin-buro) in Japan.

08 · Days 19–22

Aomori & Hirosaki

Aomori Prefecture · City & castle town

Aomori city is the end of the Shinkansen line and the gateway to Hokkaido. It's also one of Japan's best seafood cities — the Furukawa covered market (Aomori Gyosai Center) at dawn is the local alternative to Tsukiji, without the queues. The Nebuta Museum (Wa-Rasse) displays the enormous illuminated floats year-round.

Hirosaki, 40 minutes west, is a quieter version of what Kanazawa was 20 years ago. A castle still in its original form, a well-preserved samurai quarter, and an apple-growing culture that shows up in everything from cider to curry. A gentler final act before flying home.
